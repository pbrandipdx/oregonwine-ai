# SECTION 1: REGION OVERVIEW

## What is the Willamette Valley?

The Willamette Valley is Oregon's premier wine region, stretching approximately 150 miles from Portland in the north to Eugene in the south, and about 60 miles wide between the Coast Range to the west and the Cascade Range to the east. It is recognized as one of the world's premier Pinot Noir-producing areas and was named the top wine destination for 2025 by VinePair, beating out Champagne and Santorini.

The valley is home to more than 730 wineries and 930 vineyards spread across rolling hills and scenic countryside. The Willamette Valley AVA (American Viticultural Area) was officially established in 1983, thanks largely to the efforts of David Adelsheim, who petitioned the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco and Firearms for the designation.

## Climate

The Willamette Valley occupies the same latitude as Burgundy, France — approximately 45°N — placing it within the world's most favorable band for cool-climate viticulture. The Coast Range shields the valley from the most extreme Pacific maritime weather while still allowing a marine influence that keeps temperatures moderate.

Key climate characteristics include:
- Long, slow ripening seasons that develop ripe flavors while preserving natural acidity
- Cool winters that are typically mild and wet
- Rainy springs
- Warm, dry summers with cool nights that keep sugar accumulation in check
- Average growing season temperatures similar to Burgundy
- Long daylight hours during the growing season (up to 16 hours in June)
- Annual rainfall concentrated in winter months, with dry summers ideal for grape maturation
- The region avoids extreme heat while enjoying enough warmth for full ripening of cool-climate varieties

## Why the Willamette Valley Matters to Wine Lovers

If California Pinot Noir is bold and fruit-forward, and Burgundy is structured and earthy, Willamette Valley sits comfortably in between — balanced, expressive, and terroir-driven. The wines here are high in acid, relatively low in tannin, and moderate in alcohol, meaning they complement food beautifully without overpowering it. The region has earned international respect not through marketing but through blind tastings, critical acclaim, and the investment of legendary Burgundian families who recognized Oregon's potential.

# SECTION 2: HISTORY OF WILLAMETTE VALLEY WINEMAKING

## The Pioneer Era (1960s)

The story of the Willamette Valley begins with a radical idea: that this corner of Oregon could rival Burgundy in producing world-class Pinot Noir. In 1965, David Lett and Charles Coury — both UC Davis-trained winemakers — ventured north to the Willamette Valley, believing in the potential of growing cool-climate grape varieties.

In 1966, David Lett planted the first Vitis vinifera vines in the volcanic red Jory soils of the Dundee Hills. He had no way of knowing he was laying the foundation for a booming industry. At just 24 years old, armed with a degree in viticulture, another in philosophy, and 3,000 grape cuttings, Lett headed to Oregon with a firm conviction that the Willamette Valley would be the best home for Pinot Noir and Chardonnay outside of Burgundy.

Between 1965 and 1968, David Lett, Charles Coury, and Dick Erath — along with their families — established the first vineyards in the Willamette Valley. These were true pioneers, planting vines in a region where virtually no one believed fine wine grapes could grow.

## The Founding Families (1970s)

Within the next decade, a wave of passionate winemakers followed. David and Ginny Adelsheim, Ron and Marjorie Vuylsteke, Dick and Nancy Ponzi, Joe and Pat Campbell, and Bill and Susan Sokol Blosser all planted roots in the North Willamette Valley.

Key founding wineries from this era:
- The Eyrie Vineyards (1966/1970) — David and Diana Lett
- Ponzi Vineyards (1970) — Dick and Nancy Ponzi
- Sokol Blosser Winery (1971) — Bill and Susan Sokol Blosser
- Tualatin Estate Vineyard (1973) — Bill Fuller and Bill Malkmus
- Adelsheim Vineyard (1978) — David and Ginny Adelsheim

## The Moment That Changed Everything (1979)

The turning point came when David Lett entered his 1975 Eyrie Vineyards Reserve Pinot Noir in the 1979 Gault-Millau French Wine Olympiad. In a blind tasting against the finest Burgundies, Lett's wine placed in the top 10 — losing to Drouhin's 1959 Chambolle-Musigny by only two-tenths of a point. The world took notice: Oregon was a serious winemaking region.

This result was so alarming to the Burgundian establishment that Robert Drouhin organized a re-match in 1980 in Beaune. Lett's wine placed second again. The message was clear — Oregon Pinot Noir could stand alongside the best in the world.

## The Burgundy Invasion (1987-Present)

Inspired by the quality he witnessed, Robert Drouhin of Maison Joseph Drouhin purchased land in the Dundee Hills in July 1987 after attending the inaugural International Pinot Noir Celebration. Domaine Drouhin Oregon became the first Burgundy house to own and operate an estate winery in Oregon.

This long-term investment built enormous credibility and attracted other eminent Burgundian families to Oregon, including Bouchard, Louis Jadot (who acquired WillaKenzie Estate, now renamed Avery), Liger-Belair, Meo-Camuzet, and most recently Groupe Bollinger (who acquired Ponzi Vineyards in 2021). The fact that some of Burgundy's most respected names chose to invest in the Willamette Valley speaks volumes about the region's terroir.

# SECTION 3: THE 11 SUB-AVAs (AMERICAN VITICULTURAL AREAS)

The Willamette Valley contains eleven nested sub-AVAs, each with distinct terroir, soil types, microclimates, and wine characteristics.

## 3.1 Dundee Hills AVA

**Location:** South of Newberg, centered around the town of Dundee
**Established:** 2004
**Elevation:** 200-1,000 feet
**Key Soil Type:** Jory (volcanic basalt) — the famous red volcanic soil rich in iron
**Climate:** Protected, warm, with good air drainage from hillside vineyards

**Wine Character:** Dundee Hills is the birthplace of Oregon Pinot Noir. Wines from Jory soils tend to show rich, dark fruit characters — black cherry, plum — with spice notes and a distinctive mineral backbone. The volcanic soils produce concentrated, age-worthy wines.

**Notable Producers:** Domaine Drouhin Oregon, Sokol Blosser, Archery Summit, Argyle Winery, The Eyrie Vineyards, Knudsen Vineyards, Lange Estate, Domaine Serene, White Rose Estate

**Fun Fact:** When Robert Drouhin of the legendary Burgundy house tasted David Lett's Pinot Noir and decided to buy land, he chose the Dundee Hills. If the red Jory soil is good enough for Drouhin, it's good enough for anyone.

## 3.2 Yamhill-Carlton AVA

**Location:** Surrounding the towns of Yamhill and Carlton
**Established:** 2005
**Key Soil Type:** Willakenzie (ancient marine sedimentary) — some of the oldest, fastest-drying soils in the valley
**Climate:** Protected by the Coast Range, lower rainfall than other AVAs

**Wine Character:** Marine-sedimentary soils here give wines darker fruit, earthy depth, and broader silky tannins. Expect plummy black cherry, earthier minerality, and lower acidity compared to volcanic-soil AVAs. Wines tend to be structured and age-worthy.

**Notable Producers:** Ken Wright Cellars, Shea Wine Cellars, Penner-Ash Wine Cellars, Lemelson Vineyards, Resonance Wines (owned by Louis Jadot), Avery (formerly WillaKenzie Estate)

**Fun Fact:** Ken Wright was so passionate about this AVA that he personally authored the Yamhill-Carlton AVA petition. His single-vineyard bottlings are a masterclass in how different vineyard sites within the same AVA can produce dramatically different wines.

## 3.3 Eola-Amity Hills AVA

**Location:** West of Salem, between the towns of Eola and Amity
**Established:** 2006
**Key Soil Type:** Shallow, basalt-rich volcanic soils
**Climate:** Strongly influenced by the Van Duzer Corridor — a gap in the Coast Range that funnels cool Pacific Ocean winds into the valley, dropping temperatures dramatically in the afternoon

**Wine Character:** Wines from Eola-Amity Hills deliver bracing acidity and volcanic minerality. The cooling wind influence produces wines with incredible freshness, bright red fruit, and remarkable energy. The shallow basalt soils stress the vines just enough to produce small, intensely flavored grapes.

**Notable Producers:** Cristom Vineyards, Bethel Heights Vineyard, Lingua Franca, Evening Land Vineyards (Seven Springs Vineyard), Brooks Winery, Evesham Wood, Hyland Estates

**Fun Fact:** The Van Duzer Corridor winds can gust up to 25 mph, dropping afternoon temperatures by more than 35 degrees Fahrenheit. This natural air conditioning dries out vine canopies and dramatically reduces fungus pressure — making it one of the best places for sustainable farming.

## 3.4 Chehalem Mountains AVA

**Location:** North Willamette Valley, between Portland and McMinnville
**Established:** 2006
**Key Soil Types:** Three distinct types on a single landmass — basaltic (volcanic), ocean sedimentary, and loess (windblown lakebed sediment from the Missoula Floods)
**Climate:** Higher elevation with good air drainage

**Wine Character:** The three different soil types mean wines from the Chehalem Mountains can vary dramatically from site to site. Volcanic soils give power and structure, sedimentary soils give earthiness and depth, and loess soils produce the most delicate, aromatic Pinots. This diversity is a sommelier's playground.

**Notable Producers:** Adelsheim Vineyard, Rex Hill, Ponzi Vineyards, A to Z Wineworks, Chehalem Winery, Trisaetum

**Fun Fact:** Adelsheim Vineyard was the first winery established in the Chehalem Mountains, and David Adelsheim was instrumental in creating both the Willamette Valley AVA and the Chehalem Mountains AVA.

## 3.5 Ribbon Ridge AVA

**Location:** A distinct ridge within the larger Chehalem Mountains AVA
**Established:** 2005
**Size:** One of Oregon's smallest AVAs — roughly 20 vineyards across 500 planted acres
**Key Soil Type:** Ocean sedimentary (Willakenzie series)
**Climate:** Protected from marine air and heavy rains by surrounding hills, creating a warmer microclimate

**Wine Character:** Ribbon Ridge Pinot Noirs are known for their elegance and silkiness. The sedimentary soils and protected position produce wines with beautiful red fruit, fine-grained tannins, and floral notes. Chardonnay and Gamay Noir also do well here.

**Notable Producers:** Beaux Frères, Patricia Green Cellars, Brick House Vineyards, Trisaetum, Adelsheim

**Fun Fact:** Beaux Frères — co-owned by wine critic Robert Parker's brother-in-law — sits atop Ribbon Ridge and produces some of the most sought-after Pinot Noir in Oregon. The 88-acre farm follows holistic, sustainable farming principles.

## 3.6 McMinnville AVA

**Location:** Foothills of the Coast Range, west and south of McMinnville
**Established:** 2005
**Key Soil Type:** Uplifted marine sedimentary loams and silts with alluvial overlays and a base of uplifting basalt — uniquely shallow soils
**Climate:** Sheltered by the Coast Range, resulting in lower rainfall than other parts of the valley

**Wine Character:** McMinnville wines tend to show dark fruit, savory herb notes, and a firm structure. The shallow soils restrict vine vigor, producing small berries with intense concentration. Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, and Pinot Blanc all thrive here.

**Notable Producers:** Maysara Winery, Youngberg Hill, Willamette Valley Vineyards, Walnut City WineWorks

**Fun Fact:** McMinnville is also the cultural hub of Willamette Valley wine country, hosting the International Pinot Noir Celebration (IPNC) every summer — a bucket-list event for Pinot Noir lovers worldwide.

## 3.7 Van Duzer Corridor AVA

**Location:** A unique east-west gap in the Coast Range that channels Pacific Ocean air into the valley
**Established:** 2020
**Key Soil Type:** Marine sedimentary soils rich in silt, clay, and sand, with deposits of fossilized marine organisms
**Climate:** The defining feature — a natural wind tunnel. Cool ocean breezes funnel through the corridor starting as early as 2:00 PM, dropping temperatures dramatically and creating a uniquely cool growing environment.

**Wine Character:** Wines from the Van Duzer Corridor are marked by vibrant acidity, bright fruit, and a distinct freshness. The cooling winds extend the growing season, allowing grapes to develop complex flavors while maintaining excellent acid structure. This is an AVA to watch.

**Notable Producers:** Van Duzer Vineyards, Johan Vineyards, Namaste Vineyards

**Fun Fact:** The afternoon wind effect is so consistent and dramatic that vineyards here can be noticeably cooler than sites just a few miles away. Winemakers joke that you can set your watch by the Van Duzer wind.

## 3.8 Laurelwood District AVA

**Location:** Northern Willamette Valley, within the Chehalem Mountains
**Established:** 2020
**Key Soil Type:** The defining feature — Laurelwood soil series (windblown loess over ancient basalt)
**Climate:** Moderate, with good elevation and air drainage

**Wine Character:** Laurelwood soils produce wines with remarkable elegance, fine-grained tannins, and aromatic complexity. The volcanic basalt base provides mineral depth while the loess topsoil adds finesse.

**Notable Producers:** Ponzi Vineyards, Dion Vineyard

**Fun Fact:** Luisa Ponzi (second generation of Ponzi Vineyards) was instrumental in creating this AVA. The petition was submitted in 2016, and after nearly four years, the Laurelwood District became one of Oregon's newest AVAs. Groupe Bollinger — the house behind one of Champagne's most iconic brands — acquired Ponzi Vineyards in 2021, bringing French investment to this emerging AVA.

## 3.9 Tualatin Hills AVA

**Location:** Northwestern corner of the Willamette Valley, close to Portland
**Established:** 2020
**Key Soil Type:** Rich, volcanic Laurelwood soils
**Climate:** Marine-influenced with moderate temperatures

**Wine Character:** Wines from the Tualatin Hills tend toward elegance and aromatic complexity, with bright acidity and fine structure.

**Notable Producers:** Tualatin Estate Vineyard, David Hill Vineyards, SakéOne (sake producer using local rice and water)

**Fun Fact:** This is one of the closest AVAs to Portland, making it accessible for day trips from the city.

## 3.10 Lower Long Tom AVA

**Location:** West side of the Lower Long Tom Watershed, between Corvallis and Eugene
**Established:** 2021
**Key Soil Type:** Bellpine (the predominant soil series), found on stream-cut ridge lines
**Size:** 24 vineyards
**Climate:** Warmer southern end of the Willamette Valley

**Wine Character:** The southernmost AVA in the Willamette Valley, Lower Long Tom produces wines that can be slightly riper and fuller than their northern counterparts while maintaining characteristic Oregon acidity.

**Notable Producers:** Benton-Lane Winery, King Estate, Pfeiffer Winery

**Fun Fact:** King Estate — one of Oregon's largest biodynamic operations — is located here and has been a champion of Pinot Gris as well as Pinot Noir.

## 3.11 Mount Pisgah AVA

**Location:** Polk County, in the southern portion of the Willamette Valley
**Established:** 2023
**Key Soil Type:** Volcanic basalt
**Climate:** One of the newest AVAs to watch

**Wine Character:** Still emerging as a defined style, but early wines show promise with bright fruit and mineral character.

**Fun Fact:** This is the newest AVA in the Willamette Valley — a sign that the region continues to evolve and discover new terroir-driven distinctions.

# SECTION 4: GRAPE VARIETIES

## 4.1 Pinot Noir — The King of the Valley

Pinot Noir is the Willamette Valley's flagship grape, representing approximately 69% of all planted acreage. This is the grape that put Oregon on the world wine map.

### Tasting Profile
- **Aromas:** Cherry, raspberry, cranberry, violet, rose petal, tea leaf
- **Flavors:** Red cherry, dark cherry, pomegranate, plum, cola, baking spice
- **Texture:** Silky, smooth tannins with vibrant acidity
- **Signature Notes:** "Forest floor," mushroom, earth, dried herbs, wet stone
- **Body:** Medium-bodied, elegant
- **Alcohol:** Typically 12.5-14%

### How Terroir Affects Pinot Noir Flavor
- **Jory Soil (Dundee Hills):** Volcanic and iron-rich — produces wines with red fruit and spice, dark cherry concentration
- **Willakenzie Soil (Yamhill-Carlton):** Marine sedimentary — gives wines darker fruit, earthy depth, broader tannins
- **Loess (Chehalem Mountains):** Windblown and soft — leads to delicate, aromatic Pinots with floral lift
- **Basalt (Eola-Amity Hills):** Shallow volcanic — bracing acidity, bright red fruit, mineral energy

### The Major Pinot Noir Clones

Clone selection dramatically affects the character of Willamette Valley Pinot Noir. Understanding clones is key to understanding the wines.

**Heritage Clones (planted since the 1960s):**
- **Pommard:** The workhorse. Brings power, dark fruit, structure, and earthy depth. One of the first clones planted in Oregon.
- **Wadenswil (Wädenswil 1A):** The original. David Lett brought this clone from UC Davis in 1965. Known for its aromatic complexity, red fruit character, and floral lift.

**Dijon Clones (introduced in the 1980s via Oregon State University and French nurseries):**
- **Clone 113:** Early ripening, consistent yields, good color
- **Clone 114:** Similar to 113 with slightly different flavor profile
- **Clone 115:** Early ripening, intensely colored wines with concentrated fruit
- **Clone 667:** Later ripening, produces wines with great color, structure, and dark fruit
- **Clone 777:** Elegant and floral — often called the "perfume clone" for its aromatic intensity
- **Clone 828:** Less common, known for bringing additional complexity

**Modern Blending Philosophy:** Today's best winemakers combine heritage and Dijon clones to create wines with layers of complexity. Combining the power of Pommard with the elegance of Dijon 777 and the floral lift of Wadenswil can result in a wine with layers of fruit, spice, and structure. Vineyard managers adjust clonal mixes and rootstocks to match slope, aspect, and soil.

## 4.2 Chardonnay — The Rising Star

Chardonnay is experiencing a renaissance in the Willamette Valley. The region's long, cool growing season is ideally suited for producing mineral-driven, Burgundian-style Chardonnay — crisp and elegant rather than the oaky, buttery California style.

### Tasting Profile
- **Aromas:** Green apple, citrus, white flowers, wet stone
- **Flavors:** Lemon, pear, mineral, subtle toast (if oak-aged)
- **Texture:** Bright acidity, lean to medium body
- **Signature:** Mineral-driven with hints of brioche in barrel-aged versions

### Notable Chardonnay Producers
Lingua Franca, Domaine Drouhin Oregon, Cristom Vineyards, Ponzi Vineyards, Adelsheim Vineyard, Bergström Wines

## 4.3 Pinot Gris — Oregon's Signature White

Pinot Gris holds a special place in Oregon history. David Lett (Eyrie Vineyards) made the first commercial planting of Pinot Gris in North America in the late 1960s. It is now Oregon's leading white variety.

### Tasting Profile
- **Aromas:** Stone fruit, pear, honeysuckle
- **Flavors:** White peach, apple, melon, citrus zest
- **Texture:** Medium body with crisp acidity
- **Style:** Oregon Pinot Gris leans toward the Alsatian style — rounder and more textured than Italian Pinot Grigio

## 4.4 Riesling

A beautiful expression in the Willamette Valley, Riesling delivers vibrant acidity and layers of stone fruit and floral aromas. Both dry and off-dry versions are made.

### Tasting Profile
- **Aromas:** Lime, petrol (with age), jasmine, white peach
- **Flavors:** Green apple, citrus, mineral, slate
- **Texture:** Bright, racy acidity with a long finish

## 4.5 Sparkling Wine — The Next Frontier

Over the past decade, Willamette Valley winemakers have sparked a traditional method sparkling wine revolution. Oregon wineries now produce approximately 150,000 cases of traditional method sparkling wine annually. The cool climate, with its naturally high acidity, makes the Willamette Valley ideally suited for méthode traditionnelle (Champagne-method) sparkling wines.

### Key Sparkling Producers
- **Argyle Winery:** Pioneer since 1987 — the first Willamette Valley winery to establish a dedicated sparkling program. All stages are made in-house.
- **Soter Vineyards:** Long-time sparkling wine believers
- **R. Stuart & Co.:** Known for high-quality bubbles
- **Lundeen Wines:** Produces the Desirée sparkling

### Sparkling Wine Characteristics
- Méthode Traditionnelle: secondary fermentation in the bottle you open
- Notes of brioche, fresh cream, green apple, and citrus
- Extended lees contact creates toasty, complex flavors
- Both Blanc de Blancs (100% Chardonnay) and Blanc de Noirs (100% Pinot Noir) styles are produced

## 4.6 Other Varieties — The Adventurous Side

Beyond the flagship grapes, the Willamette Valley grows an impressive range of varieties:

- **Gamay Noir:** Light, juicy, vibrant — the Beaujolais grape finding a second home in Oregon
- **Grüner Veltliner:** Austria's signature grape thriving in Oregon's cool climate — peppery, crisp
- **Pinot Blanc:** Bright and refreshing, gaining popularity
- **Pinot Meunier:** The "third" Champagne grape, used in both sparkling and still wines
- **Auxerrois:** A rare Alsatian variety — floral and textural
- **Melon (Melon de Bourgogne):** The Muscadet grape — minerally and lean
- **Gewürztraminer:** Aromatic and exotic with lychee and rose petal
- **Syrah:** Fuller-bodied red, typically from warmer sites
- **Tempranillo:** Spanish variety finding expression in Oregon
- **Viognier:** Aromatic white with stone fruit and floral notes
- **Sauvignon Blanc:** Crisp and herbal
- **Müller-Thurgau:** An early-planted variety with historical significance

# SECTION 5: NOTABLE WINERIES — DETAILED PROFILES

## 5.1 The Eyrie Vineyards
- **Founded:** 1966 (first vintage 1970)
- **Founders:** David and Diana Lett
- **Current Winemaker:** Jason Lett (David and Diana's son)
- **AVA:** Dundee Hills
- **Claim to Fame:** The birthplace of Oregon wine. Produced the first Pinot Noir and first Pinot Gris in the Willamette Valley (and first commercial Pinot Gris in the United States). David Lett's 1975 Reserve Pinot Noir stunned the world at the 1979 Wine Olympiad.
- **Style:** Elegant, Burgundian, age-worthy. The Letts favor restraint and terroir expression over power.
- **Origin of Name:** Named by Diana Lett, inspired by a pair of red-tailed hawks who made their nest (eyrie) in the fir trees at the top of the vineyard.

## 5.2 Domaine Drouhin Oregon
- **Founded:** 1987
- **Founders:** Robert Drouhin (Maison Joseph Drouhin, Burgundy)
- **Current Winemaker:** Véronique Boss-Drouhin (has made 30+ vintages in both Oregon and Burgundy)
- **AVA:** Dundee Hills
- **Motto:** "French Soul — Oregon Soil"
- **Claim to Fame:** First Burgundy house to own and operate an estate winery in Oregon. Uses Burgundian techniques including imported French oak barrels and a gravity-flow winery built in 1989.
- **Wines:** Pinot Noir and Chardonnay exclusively
- **Impact:** DDO's investment gave Oregon massive international credibility and paved the way for other Burgundian houses to follow.
- **Tasting Room:** They use Burgundian winemaking techniques with imported Burgundy French Oak barrels.

## 5.3 Sokol Blosser Winery
- **Founded:** 1971
- **Founders:** Bill and Susan Sokol Blosser
- **Current Leadership:** Second generation — siblings Alex and Alison Sokol Blosser
- **AVA:** Dundee Hills
- **Estate Size:** 128+ acres under cultivation
- **Sustainability Firsts:**
  - First winery certified "Salmon-Safe" in the U.S.
  - USDA organic certification for 72-acre estate vineyard (2008)
  - First winery building with LEED certification (underground barrel cellar)
  - B Corp certified (2015)
- **Wines:** Pinot Noir, Pinot Gris, Chardonnay, Sauvignon Blanc, Rosé, and their iconic Evolution series
- **Personality:** Synonymous with sustainability — pioneers who proved you could make world-class wine while being an environmental leader.

## 5.4 Ponzi Vineyards
- **Founded:** 1970
- **Founders:** Dick and Nancy Ponzi
- **Current Ownership:** Groupe Bollinger (acquired May 2021 — the Champagne house's first property outside France)
- **AVA:** Laurelwood District
- **Key Details:**
  - Planted first vines in 1968
  - First four barrels of wine produced in 1974
  - Gravity-flow winery constructed in 2008
  - Luisa Ponzi (second generation) was instrumental in creating the Laurelwood District AVA
- **Wines:** Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Pinot Gris
- **Soil:** Laurelwood — windblown loess over ancient basalt

## 5.5 Adelsheim Vineyard
- **Founded:** 1978
- **Founders:** David and Ginny Adelsheim (purchased first 19 acres in 1971)
- **AVA:** Chehalem Mountains (first winery in this AVA)
- **Estate:** 11 estate vineyards, 200+ acres across volcanic, loess, and sedimentary soils
- **Key Contributions:**
  - David Adelsheim petitioned for and helped create the Willamette Valley AVA (1983) and the Chehalem Mountains AVA (2006)
  - Oregon Wine Board Lifetime Achievement Award (2012)
  - Grew up to 12 varieties but now focuses primarily on Pinot Noir and Chardonnay
- **Style:** Terroir-focused, elegant, refined

## 5.6 Ken Wright Cellars
- **Founded:** 1994
- **Location:** Carlton, Oregon
- **AVA:** Yamhill-Carlton (Ken Wright authored the AVA petition)
- **Specialty:** Single-vineyard Pinot Noir from 13 different vineyard sites spanning multiple sub-appellations (Yamhill-Carlton, Eola-Amity Hills, Dundee Hills, Chehalem Mountains, Ribbon Ridge)
- **Philosophy:** Each vineyard has a story, and each bottle should tell that story. The focus is entirely on site expression.
- **Tasting Notes by Site:**
  - Abbott Claim & Savoya (Yamhill-Carlton): Plummy black cherry, earthy minerality, broad silky tannins, lower acidity
  - 2023 Yamhill-Carlton AVA: Tart cherry, bramble fruits, cinnamon, clove, cola

## 5.7 Cristom Vineyards
- **AVA:** Eola-Amity Hills
- **Estate:** 240-acre farm with 65+ acres of vineyards across nine distinct vineyard blocks
- **Elevation:** 200-800 feet
- **Farming:** Organic and biodynamic principles — almost half a decade of zero synthetic inputs
- **Winemaking:** Traditional methods including whole-cluster fermentation, native yeast, no filtration
- **Wines:** Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Viognier
- **Terroir:** Patchwork of volcanic soils cooled by Van Duzer Corridor winds

## 5.8 Lingua Franca
- **Founded:** 2012
- **AVA:** Eola-Amity Hills
- **Founders:** Master Sommelier Larry Stone and David Honig
- **Winemaker:** Thomas Savre (trained in Burgundy)
- **Consulting Winemaker:** Dominique Lafon (one of Burgundy's most respected winemakers)
- **Farming:** Low-impact, organic, and biodynamic principles (guided by Bethel Heights' Mimi Casteel)
- **Wines:** Single-vineyard Pinot Noir and Chardonnay — among the most nuanced wines being made in Oregon today
- **Significance:** A Master Sommelier's dream project, bringing together the best of Burgundian tradition and Oregon terroir.

## 5.9 Beaux Frères
- **Location:** Ribbon Ridge AVA
- **Estate:** 88-acre farm with 26-acre Beaux Frères Vineyard and 9-acre Upper Terrace Vineyard
- **Farming:** Holistic, sustainable principles
- **Wines:** Super-premium Pinot Noir and Chardonnay
- **Tasting:** Classic seated experience — $65/person (waived with purchase of 2+ bottles)
- **Fun Fact:** Co-founded by Robert Parker's brother-in-law — one of the most sought-after producers in the valley.

## 5.10 Argyle Winery
- **Founded:** 1987
- **AVA:** Dundee Hills
- **Specialty:** Pioneer of méthode traditionnelle sparkling wine in the Willamette Valley
- **Claim to Fame:** First winery to establish a dedicated sparkling program with every stage made in-house
- **Wines:** Sparkling (Brut, Blanc de Blancs, Rosé), Pinot Noir, Chardonnay, Riesling

## 5.11 Willamette Valley Vineyards
- **Location:** Turner, Oregon
- **Claim to Fame:** Named "One of America's Great Pinot Noir Producers" by Wine Enthusiast Magazine
- **Tasting:** Estate Tasting Room open daily, $40/person (credited toward purchase of 2+ bottles). Open 11am-6pm (until 8pm Friday).
- **Unique Experience:** Offers a Pinot Noir Clonal Blending Experience — visitors blend their own wine from different Dijon clones
- **Wines:** Pinot Noir, Pinot Gris, Chardonnay, Riesling, and more

## 5.12 Additional Notable Producers

- **Bethel Heights Vineyard** (Eola-Amity Hills) — One of the earliest developers of the Willamette Valley scene, still at the top of its game. Mimi Casteel is recognized as a leader in biodynamic farming.

# SECTION 6: SUSTAINABILITY AND FARMING

## Oregon's Commitment to Sustainability

Oregon boasts the greatest commitment to sustainable farming of any wine region in the United States. More than half of Oregon's vineyards are farmed in a sustainable manner, as certified by independent organizations.

## LIVE Certification

LIVE (Low Input Viticulture and Enology) is Oregon's homegrown sustainability certification, developed in 1997 by an independently incorporated organization of Oregon winegrowers.

Key features of LIVE:
- Certified internationally by the International Organization for Biological Control (IOBC)
- Three-year certification process
- Uses a point system requiring continual improvement in: diversification of the agro-ecosystem, reduction of fuel use, reduction of chemical inputs (pesticides and fertilizers)
- Takes a whole-farm and whole-winery approach — the entire property including non-grape crops, landscaping, building operations, labor practices, and packaging must meet LIVE standards
- Regular third-party inspections required

## The Three Major Certification Programs

1. **LIVE (Low Input Viticulture and Enology):** Oregon's own. Whole-farm sustainability approach covering environmental and social responsibility.
2. **Organic (USDA Certified):** No synthetic pesticides, herbicides, or fertilizers. Focuses on building soil health through natural methods.
3. **Biodynamic (Demeter Certified):** The most holistic approach — treats the entire farm as a living organism. Uses biodynamic preparations to build soil health through enlivened compost. Follows astronomical calendars for planting and harvesting.

All three share fundamental principles: considering the farm as a whole system and encouraging biodiversity.

## Additional Certifications Found in the Valley
- **Salmon-Safe:** Ensures farming practices don't harm salmon habitat through runoff
- **B Corp:** Business-wide commitment to social and environmental performance
- **LEED:** Green building certification for winery structures

## Sustainability Leaders
- Sokol Blosser: B Corp, USDA Organic, Salmon-Safe, LEED-certified barrel cellar
- Cristom Vineyards: Organic and biodynamic, near-zero synthetic inputs
- King Estate: Large-scale biodynamic operation
- Lingua Franca: Organic and biodynamic principles
- Brick House Vineyards: Organic and biodynamic pioneer
- Bethel Heights: Led by Mimi Casteel, recognized biodynamic advocate

# SECTION 7: SOIL TYPES DEEP DIVE

Understanding soil is the key to understanding Willamette Valley wine. Each soil type tells a geological story millions of years in the making.

## Jory Soil
- **Origin:** Volcanic basalt from ancient lava flows (Columbia River Basalt Group)
- **Color:** Distinctive deep red
- **Characteristics:** Iron-rich, well-drained, moderately fertile
- **Where Found:** Dundee Hills (most famously), Eola-Amity Hills
- **Wine Impact:** Produces concentrated wines with dark fruit, spice, and mineral backbone
- **Depth:** Can be quite deep — allows vines to establish extensive root systems

## Willakenzie Soil
- **Origin:** Ancient marine sedimentary deposits — the valley floor was once an ancient seabed
- **Characteristics:** Clay and silt-based, well-drained, nutrient-poor (which stresses vines in a good way)
- **Where Found:** Yamhill-Carlton (most prominently), Ribbon Ridge
- **Wine Impact:** Darker fruit, earthy depth, broader silky tannins, lower acidity
- **Notable:** Some of the oldest, fastest-drying soils in the valley

## Loess
- **Origin:** Windblown sediment deposited during the Missoula Floods (~15,000 years ago)
- **Characteristics:** Fine, powdery, soft, well-drained
- **Where Found:** Chehalem Mountains, Laurelwood District
- **Wine Impact:** Delicate, aromatic Pinots with floral lift and finesse
- **Story:** During the last Ice Age, massive glacial Lake Missoula burst through an ice dam repeatedly, sending catastrophic floods through the Columbia River Gorge and depositing windblown silt across the Willamette Valley. This loess is literally dust from one of the most dramatic geological events in North American history.

## Laurelwood Soil
- **Origin:** Windblown loess over ancient basalt
- **Characteristics:** Deep, well-drained, silty
- **Where Found:** Laurelwood District AVA, Tualatin Hills
- **Wine Impact:** Elegant wines with fine-grained tannins and aromatic complexity
- **The defining soil of the Laurelwood District AVA — Ponzi Vineyards helped establish this AVA specifically because this soil type is so distinctive.

## Bellpine Soil
- **Origin:** Weathered sandstone and siltstone
- **Where Found:** Lower Long Tom AVA
- **Wine Impact:** Still being explored as this is a newer AVA

## Marine Sedimentary (General)
- **Origin:** Ancient ocean floor deposits containing fossilized marine organisms
- **Where Found:** Van Duzer Corridor, McMinnville, parts of multiple AVAs
- **Wine Impact:** Wines with silt, clay, and sand components that add texture and savory complexity
- **Fun Fact:** If you pick up a rock in some of these vineyards, you might find fossilized shells from when this area was underwater millions of years ago.

# SECTION 8: VINTAGE GUIDE

## 2018 — Outstanding
A vintage that enabled producers to make exceptional, high-quality wines across every price point and every AVA. A cool spring and normal bud break gave way to a hot, dry, sunny summer, though nights remained cool enough to preserve natural acidity. If you like bold, dark fruit-forward Pinot Noir — 2018 is your vintage. These wines have excellent aging potential and concentration.

## 2019 — Elegant and Delicate
After a great start to spring and summer, September caught everyone off-guard with storm after storm rolling in. The resulting vintage is delicate and austere, reminiscent of the early vintages of Willamette Valley Pinot Noir. These are wines for lovers of subtlety and nuance — not power. Think Burgundy rather than Napa.

## 2020 — Smoke-Affected
The devastating wildfire season across the Pacific Northwest posed challenges. Many producers either didn't make wine or carefully selected fruit that was unaffected by smoke taint. Wines that were made successfully can be excellent, but buyer caution is warranted.

## 2021 — Potential Banner Year
Warm and dry conditions created ideal growing conditions, with an early harvest of mature grapes. Some yields were down due to rain during June flowering. Wines may ultimately resemble 2018 in concentration — rich, concentrated, and age-worthy.

## 2022 — The Comeback Kid
This vintage restores faith in Mother Nature's capacity to take a poor start and finish well. A hard, killing freeze settled in during late April when buds were open and vulnerable, devastating early growth. But the season rallied. Low sugar, solid acidity, and decent flavor development produced surprisingly generous wines from the better sites, especially if picked late in October and early November.

## General Vintage Advice for Willamette Valley
- Cool vintages (like 2019) produce wines with more red fruit, higher acidity, and lighter body — ideal for those who love Burgundy
- Warm vintages (like 2018, 2021) produce wines with darker fruit, more concentration, and richer texture
- The best producers make excellent wine in any vintage — that's the mark of great winemaking
- Willamette Valley Pinot Noir can age beautifully — top wines from great vintages can cellar for 10-20+ years

# SECTION 9: SOMMELIER SERVICE GUIDE

## Serving Temperature
- **Sparkling Wine:** 38-44°F (3-7°C) — Cold but not freezing
- **White Wines (Pinot Gris, Riesling):** 45-50°F (7-10°C)
- **Chardonnay:** 50-55°F (10-13°C) — slightly warmer than other whites to allow complexity to show
- **Light Pinot Noir:** 55-60°F (13-16°C)
- **Full-bodied Pinot Noir:** 60-65°F (16-18°C)
- **Pro Tip:** Most people serve red wines too warm and white wines too cold. A slightly cool Pinot Noir will show more elegance and aromatic lift.

## Decanting
- Young Willamette Valley Pinot Noir benefits from 30-60 minutes of decanting — it smooths the wine out and can quickly age it by one to two years
- Older wines (10+ years) should be handled with more care — a gentle pour into a decanter is fine, but extended decanting may cause them to fade
- Chardonnay can also benefit from brief decanting, especially barrel-aged examples

## Glassware
- Use a Burgundy glass (large bowl, narrow rim) for Pinot Noir — it captures the delicate aromas
- A standard white wine glass works well for Pinot Gris and Riesling
- Chardonnay benefits from a slightly larger white wine glass
- Sparkling wine is best in a tulip glass (not a coupe or flute) — it concentrates aromas while allowing bubbles to develop

## Food Pairing Guide

Willamette Valley wines are naturally food-friendly because of their high acidity, moderate alcohol, and relatively low tannin.

**Pinot Noir Pairings:**
- Roasted chicken or duck — the classic pairing
- Grilled salmon or other rich fish (an Oregon natural)
- Mushroom dishes (echoing the wine's earthy notes)
- Pork tenderloin with herb crust
- Soft, bloomy-rind cheeses (Brie, Camembert)
- Charcuterie boards
- Thanksgiving dinner — Pinot Noir is the ultimate Thanksgiving wine
- Asian cuisine — the acidity and low tannin work beautifully with soy, ginger, and umami flavors
- Pizza with earthy toppings (mushroom, truffle)

**Chardonnay Pairings:**
- Roasted or grilled white fish
- Lobster, crab, or shrimp
- Creamy pasta dishes
- Roasted root vegetables
- Nutty cheeses (Gruyère, Comté)

**Pinot Gris Pairings:**
- Fresh seafood — oysters, mussels, clams
- Green salads with vinaigrette
- Light Asian dishes
- Goat cheese
- Summer vegetables

**Riesling Pairings:**
- Spicy food — Thai, Indian, Mexican (acidity cuts through heat)
- Pork and apple dishes
- Sushi and sashimi
- Fresh fruit desserts

**Sparkling Wine Pairings:**
- Anything salty or fried (the bubbles cleanse the palate)
- Oysters
- Caviar
- Fresh strawberries
- Brunch everything

**Key Pairing Principle:** It's actually the seasonings and sauces that are most significant when choosing wine. Consider whether you're pairing with a rich, creamy sauce or an intense spice element — the wine should complement, not compete.

# SECTION 10: VISITING THE WILLAMETTE VALLEY

## Getting There
- Portland International Airport (PDX) is the nearest major airport
- The northern Willamette Valley wine country (Dundee, Newberg, Carlton, McMinnville) is approximately 45-60 minutes from Portland
- Salem (central valley) is about an hour south of Portland

## When to Visit
- **Summer (June-September):** Peak season. Warm, dry weather. Long days. Most tasting rooms are open with extended hours. The busiest time — reservations recommended.
- **Harvest Season (September-October):** The most exciting time to visit. You might see grapes being picked and crushed. Fall colors are stunning.
- **Spring (April-May):** Beautiful wildflowers, fewer crowds, and many events
- **Winter (November-March):** Quieter, more intimate. Some smaller tasting rooms may have limited hours, but the major ones stay open. Great for avoiding crowds and having more personal experiences.

## Key Towns
- **Dundee:** Heart of the Dundee Hills AVA, surrounded by iconic wineries
- **Newberg:** Gateway to wine country from Portland
- **Carlton:** Small town with numerous tasting rooms within walking distance
- **McMinnville:** Cultural hub, home to the International Pinot Noir Celebration, excellent restaurants
- **Salem:** State capital, gateway to Eola-Amity Hills

## Tasting Room Etiquette
- Reservations are increasingly required, especially on weekends
- Tasting fees range from $20-$95+ per person
- Fees are often waived with wine purchase (typically 2+ bottles)
- Most tasting rooms offer seated experiences guided by knowledgeable ambassadors
- Don't be afraid to ask questions — Oregon winemakers are famously approachable and unpretentious
- Use the dump bucket — it's there for a reason and nobody judges you

- **International Pinot Noir Celebration (IPNC):** Annual summer event in McMinnville — a bucket-list gathering for Pinot lovers

# SECTION 11: FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

## "What's the difference between Oregon Pinot Noir and Burgundy?"
Oregon and Burgundy share the same latitude (45°N) and a passion for Pinot Noir, but they're distinct. Oregon wines tend to be slightly more fruit-forward while maintaining Burgundian structure and earthiness. Oregon's volcanic and sedimentary soils create different expressions than Burgundy's limestone. Think of them as cousins — related but with their own personalities.

## "Why is Willamette Valley Pinot Noir more expensive than other regions?"
Small production, low yields, and hand-harvesting all drive costs up. Most Willamette Valley wineries are small, family-owned operations. The thin-skinned Pinot Noir grape requires meticulous care in both vineyard and cellar. You're paying for craftsmanship and terroir, not brand marketing.

## "Is Oregon wine just Pinot Noir?"
Not at all! While Pinot Noir is the flagship (69% of plantings), the valley also excels with Chardonnay, Pinot Gris, Riesling, sparkling wines, and increasingly exciting wines from Gamay Noir, Grüner Veltliner, and other varieties. The Chardonnay revolution is particularly exciting — many critics say Oregon Chardonnay may eventually rival its Pinot Noir for prestige.

## "How do I pronounce Willamette?"
Will-AM-it. As locals say: "It's Willamette, dammit!"

## "What's an AVA?"
An American Viticultural Area (AVA) is a designated wine grape-growing region in the United States defined by unique geographic features — climate, soil, elevation — that distinguish it from surrounding regions. Think of it as the American equivalent of French appellations.

## "Can Willamette Valley wines age?"
Absolutely. Top Pinot Noirs from great vintages can age beautifully for 10-20+ years. The natural acidity acts as a preservative. Chardonnay can also age well, especially barrel-fermented examples. Even some Pinot Gris can develop interesting complexity with 3-5 years of age.

## "What's the best AVA?"
There is no best — each AVA produces a different expression. Dundee Hills for power and concentration, Eola-Amity Hills for freshness and energy, Yamhill-Carlton for earthiness and structure, Ribbon Ridge for elegance, Chehalem Mountains for diversity. The real fun is exploring the differences.

## "Why are there Burgundy families investing in Oregon?"
Because the terroir is world-class. When Robert Drouhin tasted David Lett's Pinot Noir in 1979 and it nearly beat his Chambolle-Musigny, he realized Oregon had something special. Maison Joseph Drouhin, Louis Jadot, Groupe Bollinger, and other prestigious French houses have all invested here — the ultimate compliment to the region's potential.

# SECTION 12: WINE TERMINOLOGY GLOSSARY

- **AVA (American Viticultural Area):** A legally designated grape-growing region defined by unique geographic characteristics
- **Biodynamic:** A holistic farming approach treating the farm as a living organism, using natural preparations and astronomical calendars
- **Blanc de Blancs:** Sparkling wine made entirely from white grapes (typically Chardonnay)
- **Blanc de Noirs:** Sparkling wine made entirely from red grapes (typically Pinot Noir)
- **Clone:** A genetically identical cutting of a grape variety, selected for specific characteristics
- **Dijon Clones:** Pinot Noir clones originating from Burgundy, numbered 113, 114, 115, 667, 777, 828
- **Estate:** Wine made from grapes grown on the winery's own property
- **Gravity-Flow Winery:** A winery designed so that grapes and wine move by gravity rather than pumps, minimizing handling
- **Jory Soil:** Volcanic basalt soil, red in color, signature of the Dundee Hills
- **LIVE:** Low Input Viticulture and Enology — Oregon's sustainability certification
- **Loess:** Fine, windblown sediment deposited by ancient floods
- **Méthode Traditionnelle:** Sparkling wine made using the same method as Champagne (secondary fermentation in bottle)
- **Missoula Floods:** Catastrophic prehistoric floods that shaped the Willamette Valley's geology
- **Native Yeast Fermentation:** Using naturally occurring wild yeasts rather than commercial cultures
- **Pommard Clone:** A heritage Pinot Noir clone known for power and dark fruit
- **Single Vineyard:** Wine produced from grapes grown in one specific vineyard site
- **Terroir:** The complete natural environment in which a wine is produced — including soil, climate, topography
- **Wadenswil Clone:** The original Pinot Noir clone brought to Oregon by David Lett in 1965
- **Whole Cluster Fermentation:** Including grape stems during fermentation, adding spice and structure
- **Willakenzie Soil:** Marine sedimentary soil found primarily in Yamhill-Carlton

## A2: Domaine Serene — Dundee Hills

**Founded:** 1989 by Ken and Grace Evenstad
**AVA:** Dundee Hills
**Estate:** Originally 42 acres of just-logged land; now one of the largest estates in the hills
**Winery:** Five-level gravity-flow facility (completed 2001), plus a separate white wine winery — the first of its kind in Oregon — dedicated exclusively to Chardonnay and sparkling wine

**Flagship Wine:** The Evenstad Reserve Pinot Noir is a sommelier favorite and has been named the #1 Oregon wine in the Wine & Spirits Restaurant Poll. The 2014 Evenstad Reserve Chardonnay received 95 points and was ranked the #2 wine in the world on Wine Spectator's Top 100 Wines of the Year.

**Style:** Ultra-premium, concentrated, polished. These wines are built for power and longevity while maintaining elegance.

## A3: Brooks Winery — Eola-Amity Hills

**AVA:** Eola-Amity Hills
**Current Owner:** Pascal Brooks (Jimi's son)

# CHUNK GROUP B: GEOLOGY & TERROIR DEEP DIVE

## B1: The Missoula Floods — How Oregon's Wine Soils Were Born

Between 15,000 and 13,000 years ago, one of the most dramatic geological events in North American history shaped the Willamette Valley's wine soils. Glacial Lake Missoula — a massive body of water in present-day Montana, held back by an ice dam — repeatedly burst through its dam and sent catastrophic floods roaring down the Columbia River Gorge.

These weren't ordinary floods. The water volume was estimated at 10 times the combined flow of all the rivers on Earth. The floodwaters backed up into the Willamette Valley, filling it like a bathtub to elevations of over 400 feet — flooding as far south as Eugene. Geologists believe there were 40 or more separate floods.

**What the floods left behind:**
- **Loess (windblown silt):** As floodwaters receded and sediments dried, winds carried fine silt particles across the valley. This loess was deposited on hillsides in the Chehalem Mountains and Laurelwood District, creating the delicate, powdery soils that produce some of Oregon's most aromatic and elegant Pinot Noirs.
- **Alluvial deposits:** The valley floor received thick layers of flood sediment, creating the rich bottomland soils that are too fertile for premium grape growing (which is why most vineyards are on hillsides, not the valley floor).
- **Erratics:** Huge boulders carried by icebergs within the floodwaters were deposited randomly across the landscape — you can still find them today, geological hitchhikers from Montana.

**Why it matters for wine:** The Missoula Floods are directly responsible for the loess soils in the Chehalem Mountains and Laurelwood District. When a winemaker says their Pinot Noir comes from "windblown loess soils," they're talking about dust from Ice Age mega-floods. That's 15,000-year-old terroir in your glass.

## B2: Volcanic Origins — Jory Soil and the Columbia River Basalt

Between 14 and 16 million years ago, massive "fissure" volcanic eruptions in eastern Washington and Oregon produced enormous volumes of Columbia River basalt. These lava flows traveled west — some flowing all the way to the Pacific Ocean through the ancestral Columbia River valley. As the basalt cooled, it formed the geological foundation of the Dundee Hills and Eola-Amity Hills.

**Jory Soil:** The iconic red soil of the Dundee Hills is derived from this ancient basalt. Jory soil is:
- Deep red in color (from iron oxide)
- Well-drained
- Moderately fertile
- Rich in minerals
- Warm (dark color absorbs heat)

**Nekia Soil:** Found in the Eola-Amity Hills, Nekia is related to Jory but shallower — sitting directly on basalt bedrock. The shallow depth restricts vine roots, stressing the vines just enough to produce small, intensely flavored grapes with higher concentration.

## B3: Ancient Ocean Floor — Marine Sedimentary Soils

Millions of years ago, much of what is now the Willamette Valley was underwater. The Coast Range was formed when a volcanic island chain collided with North America about 50 million years ago, and the sediments from that ancient ocean floor were uplifted over geological time.

**Willakenzie Soil:** Found primarily in Yamhill-Carlton and Ribbon Ridge, Willakenzie is composed of ancient marine sediments — clay, silt, and sandstone. These are some of the oldest soils in the valley and drain quickly (important for vine health).

**Fossilized marine organisms** can sometimes be found in vineyard soils — actual shells and sea creatures from when Oregon wine country was ocean floor. If that doesn't make for a great wine story, nothing does.

## B4: The Valley Between Two Ranges

The Willamette Valley exists because of a geological gap between two mountain ranges:

- **The Coast Range (west):** Formed approximately 50 million years ago when a volcanic island chain collided with the North American continent. The Coast Range shields the valley from the full force of Pacific storms while still allowing a marine influence to moderate temperatures.

- **The Cascade Range (east):** A younger, volcanic range full of active volcanoes (Mount Hood, Mount Jefferson, the Three Sisters). The Cascades block continental air masses from the east, keeping the valley's climate maritime rather than continental.

The valley between them acts as a catch basin — collecting sediments, trapping moisture, and creating a protected growing environment with the perfect balance of warmth, rainfall, and air circulation for cool-climate viticulture.

# CHUNK GROUP C: WINEMAKING EDUCATION

## C1: How Pinot Noir Is Made in the Willamette Valley

### Harvest (September-October)
Harvest decisions are the most critical of the year. Winemakers walk their vineyards daily, tasting grapes and measuring sugar levels (Brix), acidity, and phenolic ripeness. In the Willamette Valley, Pinot Noir is typically harvested between late September and mid-October, with exact timing varying by site, clone, and vintage conditions.

Most Willamette Valley Pinot Noir is **hand-harvested** — the thin-skinned Pinot grape is delicate and machine harvesting can damage the fruit. Picking crews work in the early morning when temperatures are cool to preserve freshness.

### Sorting
After harvest, grapes pass over a sorting table where workers remove damaged berries, leaves, and stems (if the winemaker is destemming). Many top producers use both hand-sorting and optical sorting machines.

### Destemming vs. Whole Cluster
This is one of the biggest stylistic decisions a Willamette Valley winemaker makes:

- **Destemmed:** Grapes are removed from their stems before fermentation. This produces fruit-forward, approachable wines with softer tannins. Most Oregon producers destem at least a portion of their fruit.

- **Whole Cluster:** Entire grape clusters — stems and all — go into the fermenter. The stems add savory, spicy, and floral complexity plus additional tannin structure. However, if stems aren't fully ripe (lignified), they can add harsh, green flavors. Oregon producers who use whole cluster typically range from 25% to 100%, depending on the vintage and their personal style.

**Notable Whole Cluster Practitioners:** Cristom Vineyards, Brick House, Evening Land

### Fermentation
- **Cold Soak (3-7 days):** Before fermentation begins, many winemakers hold the crushed grapes at cold temperatures to extract color, flavor, and aroma compounds without extracting harsh tannins.
- **Primary Fermentation (10-21 days):** Yeast converts sugar to alcohol. Oregon winemakers can use:
  - **Native/Wild Yeast:** Naturally occurring yeasts from the vineyard and winery. Slower, less predictable, but many believe it produces more complex, terroir-expressive wines. Used by Cristom, Lingua Franca, Brick House, Vincent Wine Company, and many others.
  - **Commercial Yeast:** Lab-cultured strains selected for reliability and specific flavor characteristics.
- **Punch-downs/Pump-overs:** During fermentation, the grape skins float to the top forming a "cap." Winemakers break this cap by punching it down (literally pushing it into the juice with a tool) or pumping juice from the bottom over the top. This extracts color, tannin, and flavor.

### Pressing
After fermentation, the wine is separated from the skins. Gentle pressing is important for Pinot Noir — too much pressure extracts bitter tannins.

### Malolactic Fermentation
Nearly all red wines (and many Oregon Chardonnays) undergo malolactic fermentation, where sharp malic acid (think green apple) is converted to softer lactic acid (think cream). This is what gives wines a rounder, smoother mouthfeel.

### Barrel Aging (10-18 months)
Willamette Valley Pinot Noir is aged in French oak barrels. Key variables:
- **New vs. Used Oak:** New barrels impart more vanilla, spice, and toast flavors. Oregon winemakers typically use 20-40% new oak (much less than Napa Cabernet). Too much new oak overwhelms Pinot Noir's delicate fruit.
- **Barrel Size:** Standard 225-liter Burgundy barriques are most common, but some producers experiment with larger formats (500L puncheons) for gentler oak influence.
- **Duration:** Typically 10-18 months, with longer aging for wines destined for more structure and ageability.

### Racking, Blending, and Bottling
- **Racking:** Moving wine from one barrel to another, leaving behind sediment (lees). Some producers keep their wines "sur lie" (on the lees) longer for added texture and complexity.
- **Fining and Filtration:** Some producers fine (clarify) and filter their wines before bottling. Others — like Cristom — bottle unfined and unfiltered, believing it preserves more character. Both approaches can produce excellent wine.
- **Blending:** For wines labeled by AVA or "Willamette Valley," the winemaker blends barrels from different vineyard blocks to create the final blend. Single-vineyard wines skip this step.

## C2: Understanding Oak in Oregon Pinot Noir

**French Oak vs. American Oak:**
Oregon Pinot Noir is aged almost exclusively in French oak. French oak is tighter-grained than American oak, imparting subtler flavors — vanilla, baking spice, and a slight toast character that complements rather than dominates the fruit. American oak (common in Napa Cabernet and bourbon) gives more aggressive coconut and dill notes that can overwhelm Pinot Noir.

**The Cooperage Matters:**
Different French cooperages (barrel makers) — François Frères, Damy, Remond, Cadus — produce barrels with distinct characters. Winemakers choose cooperages as carefully as they choose clones, and many use a mix of cooperages in each vintage.

**The Trend:** Oregon is moving toward less new oak overall. Twenty years ago, 40-50% new oak was common. Today, many top producers use 20-30% new oak, and some have moved to mostly neutral (used) barrels to let the fruit and terroir speak more clearly.

## C3: What Does "Gravity Flow" Mean?

Many premium Willamette Valley wineries (Domaine Drouhin, Domaine Serene, Archery Summit, Ponzi, Penner-Ash) are designed as "gravity-flow" facilities. Instead of using mechanical pumps to move grapes and wine through the winemaking process, the buildings are built on multiple levels so that gravity does the work.

**How it works:**
1. Grapes arrive at the top level and are sorted
2. They fall by gravity into fermentation tanks on the next level down
3. After fermentation, wine flows by gravity into barrels in the cellar below

**Why it matters:** Pumping wine can be rough — it introduces oxygen and can break up delicate Pinot Noir skins and juice. Gravity is gentler, producing wines with finer tannins and more nuanced flavors. It's an investment in quality that visitors can literally see when they tour these multi-level facilities.

# CHUNK GROUP G: WINE EDUCATION — TASTING LIKE A PRO

## G1: How to Taste Wine (for beginners)

**Look:** Hold the glass against a white background. Pinot Noir should be translucent — you should be able to see through it. Deeper color often means warmer vintage or more concentrated winemaking.

**Swirl:** Gently swirl the glass to release aromas. This isn't pretentious — it actually works. Swirling introduces oxygen and volatilizes aromatic compounds.

**Smell:** Stick your nose deep into the glass. What do you get? Fruit (cherry, raspberry)? Flowers (violet, rose)? Earth (mushroom, forest floor)? Spice (cinnamon, clove)?

**Sip:** Take a small sip and let it coat your mouth. Notice:
- **Acidity:** Does it make your mouth water? (Willamette Valley wines are naturally high in acid — this is a feature, not a bug)
- **Tannin:** Does it dry your gums? (Pinot Noir should have fine, silky tannins, not rough ones)
- **Body:** Light, medium, or full? (Oregon Pinot is typically medium-bodied)
- **Finish:** How long do the flavors linger? Longer finish = higher quality

**The best advice:** Trust your palate. If you like it, it's good wine. There are no wrong answers.

## G2: Willamette Valley Tasting Vocabulary

Common descriptors for Willamette Valley Pinot Noir and what they actually mean:

- **"Forest floor":** That earthy, damp-leaves, mushroomy quality that comes from volcanic and sedimentary soils. It's terroir you can taste.
- **"Bright acidity":** The wine makes your mouth water and feels lively, not flat. This is the Van Duzer wind and cool climate at work.
- **"Silky tannins":** The texture is smooth, like silk, not grippy or drying. This is what well-made Pinot Noir does.
- **"Mineral":** A stony, wet-rock quality — hard to define but you know it when you taste it. Often associated with volcanic soils.
- **"Whole cluster spice":** Peppery, cinnamon-like spice notes that come from fermenting with grape stems intact.
- **"New oak":** Vanilla, toast, baking spice flavors from aging in new French oak barrels.
- **"Terroir-driven":** The wine tastes like the place it came from, not like a winemaker's recipe. The ultimate compliment in Oregon.

## G3: The Difference Between $20 and $80 Oregon Pinot Noir

**$15-25 range (A to Z, Willamette Valley Vineyards appellation blend):**
- Blended from multiple vineyard sources across the valley
- Fruit-forward, approachable, ready to drink tonight
- Often destemmed with some new oak
- Great everyday wine

**$35-55 range (AVA-designated, smaller producers):**
- Sourced from a specific sub-AVA (Dundee Hills, Eola-Amity Hills, etc.)
- More site-specific character — you can taste the AVA
- More complex winemaking (some whole cluster, native yeast)
- Can age 5-10 years

**$60-100+ range (single-vineyard, top producers):**
- From one specific vineyard block
- Hand-sorted, small-lot production
- Complex winemaking: whole cluster, native yeast, extended aging
- Profound terroir expression — you're tasting the exact hillside
- Can age 10-20+ years
- Produced in tiny quantities (often 200-500 cases)
